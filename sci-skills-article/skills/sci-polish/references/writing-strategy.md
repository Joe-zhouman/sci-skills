# Writing Strategy

Core principles that govern all paragraph- and section-level rewriting. Load this for every polish job.

## Core stance

Academic polishing is not only about style. It is about making the reasoning legible. A polished paragraph that still performs the wrong rhetorical job is a failed edit.

- Language serves argument. Do not polish sentences while leaving the reasoning broken.
- Write with empathy for the reader: relevance first, then novelty, then trust, then reuse, then meaning.
- Do not invent data, references, mechanisms, or novelty claims.
- Do not let AI draft the paper's core scientific argument from scratch.
- If the draft is structurally rough, reconstruct the logic first and the prose second.

## Hourglass structure

Most strong research writing follows a `broad → narrow → broad` pattern:

- **Introduction**: open the territory, narrow to the gap, then state the study
- **Discussion/Conclusion**: start from the specific findings, widen to implications and limits

If a paragraph or section violates this architecture, rebuild it before polishing wording.

## Writing order is not reading order

The author may draft in one order and the reader may consume in another. A productive planning sequence:

1. Results
2. Introduction and Conclusion
3. Title
4. Discussion
5. Materials and Methods
6. Abstract

Organize around evidence and argumentative function, not the chronology of the raw draft.

## Claim, evidence, boundary

Every important scientific statement should have three parts:

1. **claim**: what is being said
2. **evidence**: what supports it
3. **boundary**: where the claim stops, or what uncertainty remains

Typical failures:

| Failure | Example |
|---|---|
| Claim without evidence | "Our method is superior" — no comparison data |
| Data without a claim | "Figure 3 shows a 12% increase" — so what? |
| Implication without scope | "This approach can be applied broadly" — in what contexts? |
| Correlation → causation | "X correlates with Y, therefore X causes Y" |

When polishing, repair these failures before polishing rhythm.

## Section responsibilities

### Introduction

Should answer four questions:
1. What is already known?
2. What remains unresolved?
3. What exact question does this study ask?
4. How does the study address it?

Do not summarize Results or Conclusions here.

### Results

State what was observed. Provide: object/system, condition, quantitative support, direct result. Stay mainly in past tense. Do not turn Results into Discussion by adding long mechanistic interpretation.

### Discussion

Explain what the findings mean:
- How the work fits the broader field
- What has been added to understanding
- Which earlier work is supported, revised, or complicated
- Which explanations are plausible
- Which limitations constrain the interpretation

Discussion is the natural home for hedging.

### Methods

Should pass a reproducibility test: could another group repeat the work from this description (plus cited protocols)? Reject vague writing: "under standard conditions", "using routine methods", "data were analysed statistically".

### Conclusion

Not a mini-Discussion. A strong closing:
1. Restates the central contribution
2. Identifies the decisive evidence
3. States the implication with a boundary

Do not introduce new data here.

### Abstract

A mini-paper: context/problem → gap → approach → key results → implication. Should help the reader decide whether the paper is relevant, credible, and potentially important.

## Citation as positioning

Citation tells the reader how the current work stands relative to earlier work:

- **support**: prior work supports the premise
- **borrow**: current work adopts a method, framework, or protocol
- **contrast**: current work differs in result, setting, or interpretation
- **reuse/adaptation**: material, data, code, or images come from elsewhere

Always cite the source actually read and verified. Do not cite a paper as direct support if you only know it through another paper's summary.

## Fairness to earlier work

Do not manufacture novelty by flattening previous studies into a weak baseline. Prefer:

- "Although previous studies showed ..., their performance in ... remains unclear."
- "Earlier work established ..., but did not address ..."

This preserves intellectual honesty while still making the gap explicit.

## Diagnose failure mode before editing

Before rewriting, identify the main problem. See SKILL.md Step 1 for the full diagnosis hierarchy:

```
paper type logic → section job → paragraph structure → claim/evidence/boundary → sentence polish
```

Fix in this order. Do not sentence-polish a draft whose section job is wrong.

## Terminology consistency

On first contact with a draft, build a Terminology Ledger: list key terms, abbreviations, units, and notation, pick one canonical form per term, and enforce it across every section. Do not introduce synonyms to vary the prose — in academic writing, consistency > variety.
