# Section Guide

Per-section polishing priorities, failure modes, and syntax patterns. Load the relevant section(s) for each job.

## Abstract

**Job**: mini-paper — context/problem → gap/objective → approach → key results → implication.

**Polishing priorities**:
- Cut sentences that summarize background the title already implies
- Make the gap and the contribution one short, locatable sentence each
- The last sentence should state significance, not repeat the result
- Follow journal-specific abstract format if it conflicts with the generic pattern

**Common failure modes**:
- Background overwhelms the gap and contribution
- Results listed without synthesis
- Last sentence is a vague "implications are discussed"

## Introduction

**Job**: tell the reader why the work matters, what gap it fills, and what question it answers.

**Polishing priorities**:
- Opening paragraph must position, not textbook-summarize
- The gap must be explicitly named, not implied
- The transition from "what is known" to "what this paper does" must be clear
- Do not summarize Results or Conclusion here
- Do not preview Methods in detail

**Common failure modes**:
- Opening reads as a textbook chapter, not a positioning move
- Gap is never explicitly stated
- The paper's question is buried in background
- Methods previewed at length (belongs in Methods)

## Results

**Job**: report what was observed — object, condition, quantitative support, direct result.

**Polishing priorities**:
- Stay mainly in past tense
- Report observations, not interpretations
- Use statistics correctly and sparingly
- Avoid citing supplementary data when the result should stand in main text

**Syntax**: Results sentences use reporting verbs — "was detected", "increased", "showed", "enabled", "achieved". Do not drift into Discussion syntax ("may reflect", "suggests that", "is likely due") unless the transition is intentional.

**Common failure modes**:
- Interpreting findings inline instead of in Discussion
- Vague comparisons ("higher than control") without effect size or test
- Citing supplementary data for main-text claims

## Discussion

**Job**: explain what the findings mean — fit in field, contribution, comparison to prior work, interpretation, limitations.

**Polishing priorities**:
- Start from specific findings, widen to implications
- Address rival explanations, not just the favored one
- Credit earlier work fairly
- State when the interpretation may fail
- Match hedging strength to evidence strength

**Syntax**: Discussion sentences interpret — "may reflect", "suggests that", "could indicate", "is likely due to", "may facilitate".

**Common failure modes**:
- Re-summarizing Results instead of interpreting them
- Skipping rival explanations
- Omitting boundaries (when does the interpretation stop holding?)
- Promoting "consistent with" to "demonstrates"

## Conclusion

**Job**: restate contribution, identify decisive evidence, state implication with boundary.

**Polishing priorities**:
- Not a mini-Discussion — be selective
- One clear central contribution
- Implication paired with scope condition
- Do not introduce new data or claims

**Common failure modes**:
- Repeating the Discussion in shorter form
- Adding new claims not supported by the presented evidence
- Vague "this has important implications" without saying what or for whom

## Title

**Job**: signal the paper's contribution in one line.

**Polishing priorities**:
- Include key terms for discoverability
- Prefer declarative over descriptive when the evidence supports it
- Keep ≤ 75 characters for Nature-style journals
- Avoid wasted words: "A study of...", "Investigations into..."

**Common failure modes**:
- Too vague to be informative
- Too long (buried lede)
- Claim stronger than the evidence supports

## Methods

**Job**: enable reproducibility — another group could repeat the work from this description plus cited protocols.

**Polishing priorities**:
- Reject vague wording: "under standard conditions", "using routine methods", "data were analysed statistically"
- Cite protocols and prior methods explicitly
- Keep past tense for what was done
- Distinguish new methods from adaptations of existing ones

**Common failure modes**:
- Vague descriptions that can't be reproduced
- Missing key parameters (concentrations, temperatures, durations, sample sizes)
- Mixing results with methods
