# Cover Letter Writing Guide

> Apply these every time you write a cover letter. Not run-once — run every paragraph through these checks.

## Background hook

**Don't summarize the field. Don't write a mini-review.** The editor decides in 30 seconds. First sentence: why now. Best opening is the convergence of two trends — "A and B both made breakthroughs recently, but nobody has put A and B together yet."

**Signal audience, don't announce it.** "This work should interest researchers in..." is a wasted sentence. If your background paragraph naturally mentions computational materials, ML, and 2D materials, readers in those fields will recognize themselves. Show the audience through content, not declaration.

## Findings summary

**Numbers numb editors. Stories make them curious.** "Model achieves r=0.98 on test set" means nothing to an editor. "Structures with identical vacancy counts but different spatial arrangements differ in predicted resistance by a factor of two" — that's a story. Editors don't evaluate statistics (reviewers do). Editors evaluate: "is this interesting?" Numbers don't answer that question.

**One paragraph, one finding.** If you're writing a second paragraph, you're explaining method instead of reporting discovery. Method details belong in the manuscript.

## Journal fit

**This is the most-botched section.** Most cover letters say: "Your journal is prestigious and publishes important work. Our manuscript is a good fit." Information content: zero.

A good journal-fit section should make the editor feel you actually read their journal. Cite 3-4 recent papers from this journal specifically. Don't list them — thread them: "In 2021, X et al. published A in this journal. In 2024, Y et al. extended this to B. Our work pushes this trajectory to C." Show your manuscript is a continuation of an existing scientific conversation in this journal.

**Never cite papers from other journals in this section.** Even if they're more relevant. This paragraph does one job: tell the editor "your readers are already on this path, we're taking the next step."

## Tone

**Confident, not boastful.** "This is the first study to..." — make sure it actually is. If three similar papers exist, narrow the scope: "This work provides the first systematic investigation of..."

**Avoid empty adjectives.** "novel", "groundbreaking", "unprecedented", "revolutionary" — these words have been diluted to zero semantic value in cover letters. More than one will trip the editor's bullshit filter. Let the findings speak.

## Adapting for a different journal

**Don't copy-paste and swap journal names.** Editors can tell. The background paragraph may need rewriting — different journals have different readerships, same work appeals from different angles. The journal-fit paragraph almost certainly needs a full rewrite — you're citing different papers from a different journal.

**Keep what works.** The findings summary usually doesn't need major changes — your discovery is the same. But the hook and the journal-fit sections should be reconsidered from scratch.

## Revision cover letter

**Don't re-argue the paper.** It already went through review. The revision cover letter tells the editor three things: (1) formatting changes (template, language editing), (2) non-scientific changes (authors, funding, data statements), (3) everything else is in the Response Letter.

**Length: 1/3 to 1/2 of a first-submission letter.** If you're past one page, you're doing something wrong.
